Project Idea

Overview

Most people who have experienced working from home during the ongoing pandemic are aware of how easy it is, free from distractions, to become lost in one’s work for hours at a time. Rather than visit someone else’s desk for a chat or to make an enquiry, workers simply type messages to another, or use video conferencing tools. Instead of walking to the station, or down the street for lunch, people are shuffling to their fridges for a snack.

My project aims to alleviate some of health risks posed by the sedentary nature of working from home, by providing a desktop monitoring system that measures a worker’s movement and his or her workspace’s ambient lighting. Based on the data collected, the system sends alerts and suggestions to the user’s phone, all aimed at improving the user’s habits and, thus, overall health. For example, if the system detects a user has been sitting too long, it sends an alert to his or her phone encouraging them to stand, providing a set of simple stretching exercises to follow. If it detects an insufficient level of light in the room, it will automatically adjust the lighting if smart lights are available, or will otherwise provide advice to the user.

Motivation

Since the advent of the COVID-19 pandemic in March 2020, many Australians have been forced, or chosen, to work remotely. During the peak of the lockdown, some 32% of the workforce was working from home, and this percentage was much higher for employees who worked in offices (Fisher, 2021). Even following the control of the spread of COVID-19 and vaccine rollouts, it remains likely many people will continue to work from home: in fact, 47% of employers are amenable to the idea, while up to 60% of employees want to work remotely for at least part of each week (Black, 2020).

Aside from the obvious benefits of remote working, such as a reduction in commute times and more time with family, several negative consequences have arisen. Of those surveyed about their experiences of working from home, 20% reported their physical health was “worse or much worse” than prior to lockdown (ABS, 2021); a separate study by the CSIRO found almost 40% of people believed they had gained weight during the period of restrictions (Brindal, 2021), and 40-50% claimed their daily screen-time activities had increased (Brindal, 2021). Perhaps most worryingly, there is evidence to suggest work-life balance was also interrupted, with the average worker now conducting 5.24 hours of unpaid overtime each week, which represents an increase of 0.6 hours from 2019 (Black, 2020).

Recently, the effects of lighting quality on workers have been documented. Studies suggest that harsh or inadequate lighting adversely affects workers’ moods, reducing people’s productivity and wellness (OSW, 2019). This issue is clearly of concern for those working from home, where lighting would rarely be suitably designed to meet the standards of a professional workspace.

As the working landscape rapidly changes, we need to find ways to counter these trends to ensure the nation’s long-term health does not suffer. Clearly, then, there is a need for a means of altering remote workers’ habits that is easy to use, requires little input from users, draws upon the latest health advisories, and is cost-effective.

Description

The system will consist of a small box containing the necessary hardware, which can be placed in a location convenient for the user, no more than three metres from his or her smartphone if using Bluetooth, or simply within range of the user’s network if connecting with Wi-Fi. Motion sensors attached to the enclosure are placed on the underside of the user’s desk, while the light sensor needs to be attached to the top of the user’s monitor.

As outlined by Safe Work Australia, any more than seven hours of sedentary behaviour per day is considered detrimental to one’s health. To avoid this, workers are recommended to break up periods of sitting with standing and walking, ideally as illustrated in the final time block below:

Sourece:https://www.safeworkaustralia.gov.au/sedentary

To this end, the proposed system will send an alert to a user every time it detects he or she has been sitting for longer than 45 minutes. At sixteen intervals, the system will prompt the user to go for a short walk or stand, and will suggest a longer walk in the middle of the day. Eight times a day, the system will ask the user to report his or her water consumption, so intake can be monitored across the day. In six of these intervals, the system will also provide the user with varied sets of simple stretching exercises to undertake. If users are finding the interruptions occur too frequently, she can choose to ‘snooze’, or may customise the regime to suit her personal preferences. Users can access daily and weekly summaries of their activity levels based on the suggestions they have accepted, including an estimation of the calories they have burned. Finally, users can set targets for the percentage of the day they wish to be active, which will automatically adjust the number and nature of prompts sent by the systems. These features are all designed to instil healthy habits into workers over time.

Occupational Health and Safety Australia recommends a light output between 320 and 400 lux for ‘Moderately Difficult Visual Tasks’ such as routine office work (OHSA, 2020). As such, the system will alert users if the ambient light level falls outside this range, and automatically adjusting the light intensity if smart lights are installed.

Tools and Technologies

To reduce costs, the system will run on a Raspberry Pi 3 B+ (RRP $62.65), which features built-in Bluetooth and Wi-Fi for connectivity, using the open-source OS Raspbian. Two ultrasonic module distance measuring sensors ($3.85 each) will be employed to detect the presence of a user at his or her desk, and a light-dependent resistor sensor ($4.79 for two) will measure the light level. All sensors will be attached to the Raspberry Pi’s GPIO pins, and Python scripts will be used to interpret the raw data and send appropriate alerts to the user’s phone. An Android app will receive these alerts and make suggestions where required.

Skills Required

All the required hardware is readily available and cheap. Raspian can be downloaded free of cost and is relatively simple to install and update. I will need to learn more about Python to complete this project, but there are numerous online tutorials and examples that employ similar hardware, so this should be achievable. In addition, an understanding of Java will be required to develop the Android app. If time is limited, in lieu of smartphone integration a series of LEDs and a simple speaker could be attached to the Raspberry Pi to interact with the user.

Outcome

This project has the potential to improve the health of work-from-home employees. It will reduce the time workers are sedentary by gently encouraging them to exercise more and forming habits that will benefit them into the future, and possibly for their entire working lives. This will counter the emerging trend of unhealthy behaviours in remote workers, and hopefully address the issue of the rise in hours of unpaid work by making workers more aware of the need for work-life balance. By helping to identify deficiencies in lighting output, the system may also provide benefits to the mood of workers, thus leading to boosts in productivity. These improvements at an individual level could have flow-on affects for the nation, with less workers absent due to illness, and an increase in overall productivity.
